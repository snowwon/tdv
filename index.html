( The Design Village · Creative Offsite )

Digital literacy for the elderly

Five days of field immersion, workshops and shared meals in the hills of the Oltrepò Pavese — an international research team living and working from Villa‑Castello di Torrazzetta to understand how the elderly of Borgo Priolo navigate an increasingly digital world.

17–22 May 2026 Torrazzetta · Borgo Priolo · Pavia · Milan 10 researchers · 7 institutions

( Why we came )

A village of 1,300, solving the digital world on its own

Borgo Priolo is a rural community in Lombardy where the younger generation has largely moved away and the elderly have stayed behind. With roughly 1,300 residents, older adults here must often resolve digital challenges — banking, paying tax, public services, and now AI — by themselves.

This exploratory study asks how relationships of dependency and learning work among peers and across generations, and examines the “systemicality” of the current environment — its context, interactions and products/services — to assess its sustainability and to spot the weak signals that could lead to innovative design interventions. Grounded in field immersion and stakeholder dialogue, the inquiry moves beyond skills‑based literacy toward questions of dignity, trust and systemic care.

The offsite is deliberately the first step of a longer‑term relationship: The Design Village and its collaborators intend to keep engaging the community, co‑create coping strategies, and measure how things evolve over time.

Research questions

  • What are the dynamics of dependency and learning — between peers and across generations — and what environments and in‑person relationships facilitate digital engagement?
  • Is the systemicality (context, interactions, products/services) of the current situation sustainable, and which weak signals point to innovative products and services?
  • What contribution can design make to support the elderly toward autonomy and coping strategies that benefit their wellbeing?

( The workshop )

From grounding to weak signals, in five phases

The workshop was structured as a five‑phase arc — moving from shared knowledge, to a common problem statement, to systems mapping, to the field, and finally to synthesis and futures.

Preparation · Masterclasses

An opening masterclass by Marieke Sonneveld, with faculty case studies giving each researcher's lens on the territory.

Preparation · Framing talks

Andre Liem on placemaking, Ruonan Huang on autonomy as a key variable in digital literacy, and Kate Sangwon Lee on designing for and with communities.

Preparation · Materials

Printed territorial maps, sticky‑note systems, and local materials — clay, terracotta, fabric, natural fibres — for co‑design prototyping.

01

Context, literature & grounding

Guided walks to establish slowness and observation; collective review of literature, policies and case studies on digital literacy and ageing; reflections on global and India‑specific contexts of fraud, exclusion and care systems.

Focus — grounding the inquiry in knowledge, place and lived realities.

02

Framing the problem

Facilitated discussions to define the core academic and social challenge; moving beyond skills‑based literacy to dignity, autonomy, trust and care; aligning on a shared problem statement with systemic and ethical concerns.

Focus — clarifying what truly needs to be addressed.

03

Stakeholder & systems mapping

Mapping elderly users, families, financial institutions, platforms, governments and caregivers; identifying points of vulnerability, responsibility and power; exploring how trust and dependency are produced or broken.

Focus — seeing digital literacy as a shared, systemic responsibility.

04

Field immersion & research questions

Town immersion in Voghera to observe everyday interactions of ageing adults; studying non‑digital trust systems — banks, pharmacies, public offices, informal support networks; translating observation into focused research questions.

Focus — anchoring research questions in lived, civic realities.

05

Futures, funding & synthesis

A morning studio session of collaborative making with local materials; afternoon peer dialogues exchanging feedback on direction and process; evening group reflection on how cultures and methods intersect — closed by a chef's table dinner celebrating the week's learnings.

Focus — moving from reflection to continuity and action.

( Early findings )

Weak signals from a strong community

The offsite was designed as exploratory research — its outputs are directions and signals to guide follow‑up studies, not conclusions. Four threads emerged across the mapping, the field visits and the community dialogues.

Signal 01

Trust lives in non‑digital systems

Banks, pharmacies, public offices and informal support networks still carry the trust that digital services assume. Any intervention has to build from these existing trust infrastructures rather than around them.

Signal 02

Dependency is a relationship, not a deficit

Learning flows between peers and across generations in specific, personal ways. Who helps whom — and at what social cost — matters as much as which skills are missing.

Signal 03

Beyond skills, toward dignity

The shared problem statement moved past skills‑based literacy to questions of dignity, autonomy, trust and systemic care — reframing what "digital literacy" should even mean for this community.

Signal 04

Placemaking is the vehicle

Craft heritage, agritourism and the village's public spaces are where engagement already happens. Co‑design with local materials — clay, fibres, terracotta — grounds digital questions in the places people already trust.

These are preliminary, exploratory observations from one week of immersion. They will guide the follow‑up research plan as The Design Village builds a longer‑term relationship with Borgo Priolo.
